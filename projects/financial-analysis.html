Project 01
Financial analytics · Operations

Revenue & Margin
Analysis

Multi-location P&L tracking, cost driver analysis and pricing optimisation for a food retail and catering business. Built financial models and reporting workflows that contributed to a 13 percentage point gross margin improvement over eight quarters.

Tools: Python · SQL · Excel · Power BI
Period: 2019 – 2023
Scale: 3 locations · 8 quarters
01 — Overview
The problem

Running three food retail locations in Shanghai meant dealing with fragmented data across delivery platforms, supplier invoices and point-of-sale systems. Financial visibility was low — there was no consolidated P&L view, no systematic tracking of cost drivers, and pricing decisions were made on instinct rather than data.

The goal was to build an analytical foundation: collect and unify revenue, cost and order data, identify what was driving margin erosion, and use those insights to make better operational and pricing decisions.

02 — Methodology
How the analysis was built

Data was extracted from delivery platform APIs and internal records using SQL, then cleaned and unified in Python (pandas). A P&L model was built in Excel with Power BI dashboards for ongoing monitoring. Cost categories were broken down into COGS, platform commissions, labour and operations to enable root-cause analysis of margin changes.

Competitor pricing was scraped periodically for benchmarking. Pricing decisions were validated using elasticity estimates derived from historical order volume and revenue data.

04 — Results
Key findings
Platform fees were the biggest drag
Commission rates of 18–22% on delivery platforms accounted for 40% of margin erosion. Renegotiation + promoting direct ordering channels contributed 4pp.
Product mix shift added 3pp
SKU-level profitability analysis revealed high-volume, low-margin items. Adjusting the menu mix toward higher-margin categories improved blended margin without losing volume.
Labour scheduling optimisation
Workload data showed overstaffing during off-peak hours. A scheduling model based on order patterns reduced labour cost by 8% — contributing 3pp to gross margin.
Dynamic pricing on peak periods
Weekend and lunchtime orders showed low price sensitivity. A modest pricing premium during peak hours contributed the final 3pp with no measurable drop in conversion.
